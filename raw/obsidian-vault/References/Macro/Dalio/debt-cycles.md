# Debt Cycles — 3 Types

> Source: Ray Dalio, *Principles for Navigating Big Debt Crises* (2018); *Principles for Dealing with the Changing World Order* (2021)
> This document is an analytical framework tool. Not investment advice.

---

## Overview

Dalio identifies three distinct debt cycle types operating at different timescales. They interact — a short-term credit crunch can trigger a long-cycle debt crisis; a long-cycle collapse can destroy the reserve currency and trigger a currency cycle transition. Understanding which stage of each cycle the current environment occupies is the core of Dalio's macro investment framework.

---

## Cycle 1 — Short-Term Debt Cycle (~5-8 Years)

### Mechanism
Also called the business cycle or credit cycle. Driven by central bank policy and credit availability.

**The engine**: Credit growth → spending → income → more credit → eventually inflation or asset bubbles → central bank tightens → credit contracts → recession → central bank eases → repeat.

**Key players**: Central bank (interest rate setter), commercial banks (credit multipliers), households and corporations (borrowers/spenders).

### Stages

#### 1A — Early Recovery
- Credit cheap and available; borrowing accelerates
- Employment rising; wage growth moderate
- Asset prices recovering from prior trough
- Inflation low; central bank accommodative
- **Indicators**: PMI expanding, credit growth > GDP growth, unemployment falling, 2yr/10yr yield curve steep (normal)

#### 1B — Mid-Cycle Expansion
- Economy running at or near capacity
- Asset prices elevated; earnings growing
- Credit conditions tightening gradually
- Inflation approaching central bank target
- **Indicators**: CAPE rising, ISM > 55, unemployment near NAIRU, yield curve flattening

#### 1C — Late Cycle / Overheating
- Capacity constraints visible (labor shortages, supply chain pressure)
- Inflation above target
- Central bank actively hiking
- Credit conditions tightening
- **Indicators**: Inverted yield curve (2yr > 10yr), CPI > 3%, credit spreads widening, PMI topping

#### 1D — Contraction / Recession
- Credit tightens; spending falls; earnings miss
- Unemployment rising
- Asset prices falling
- **Indicators**: ISM < 50, unemployment rising, credit spreads wide, yield curve re-steepening (from bottom)

#### 1E — Trough / Stimulus
- Central bank cuts rates (or exhausts conventional tools → QE)
- Government stimulus deployed
- Asset prices bottom and recover
- Next cycle begins

### Crisis Triggers
- Policy error: central bank too slow to hike (allows inflation) or too aggressive (crushes growth)
- External shock (oil price, pandemic, financial contagion)
- Asset bubble burst (always credit-enabled)

### Indicators Per Stage
| Stage | Yield Curve | PMI | Credit Spreads | Unemployment | CPI |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Early recovery | Steep | Rising, < 55 | Narrow | Falling | Low |
| Mid expansion | Flattening | 55-60 | Narrow | Near NAIRU | At target |
| Late cycle | Flat/inverting | Topping | Widening | Very low | Above target |
| Contraction | Inverted then steep | < 50 | Wide | Rising | Falling |
| Trough | Steep | Bottoming | Wide then narrow | Peaked | Low |

### Historical Examples
- **US 2001 recession**: Tech bubble burst → Fed cuts → 2003-2007 expansion → 2008 GFC
- **US 2008-2009**: GFC → zero rates + QE → 2010-2019 expansion → COVID shock
- **US 2020**: COVID recession → massive stimulus → 2021-2022 inflation spike → 2022-2024 hiking cycle

### Current Position (2024-2026 estimate)
Dalio framework application: US likely in late Stage 1C/1D transition (2024). Fed rate hikes completed; yield curve un-inverting; unemployment beginning to rise. Early signs of entry into new accommodation cycle. *Not a prediction — framework application only.*

## Cycle 2 — Long-Term Debt Cycle (~50-75 Years)

### Mechanism
The long cycle is driven by the secular accumulation of debt across many short cycles. Each short cycle trough adds more debt (stimulus creates debt that is never fully repaid). Over 50-75 years, the total debt burden reaches a point where it cannot be serviced without debt monetization, default, or prolonged deflation.

**The engine**: Debt accumulates faster than income (GDP) over decades. When the debt service burden exceeds the economy's capacity to generate income, the long cycle peaks. Deleveraging is painful and typically involves some combination of:
1. Austerity (spending cuts / tax hikes) — deflationary
2. Debt restructuring/default — deflationary
3. Money printing / debt monetization — inflationary
4. Redistribution (wealth transfer) — social/political

### Stages

#### 2A — Early Accumulation (decades 1-2 of cycle)
- Debt/GDP rising from low base; growth exceeds debt service
- Interest rates declining (Dalio's "beautiful deleveraging" conditions)
- Asset prices rising with economy
- Central bank has full toolkit available

#### 2B — Mid-Cycle Expansion (decades 2-4)
- Debt becomes normalized; financial innovation creates leverage
- Asset prices increasingly debt-driven
- Inequality widening (capital returns > wage growth)
- Central bank still able to stimulate each short cycle

#### 2C — Late Accumulation / Peak (decades 4-6)
- Debt/GDP at historical highs (100-150%+ for government; 300%+ total)
- Central bank interest rates near zero — running out of conventional tools
- QE introduced; balance sheet expansion
- Each successive stimulus has diminishing real economy effect
- Zombie firms and zombie government entities survive on cheap money
- Asset bubbles persistent and widespread

#### 2D — Crisis / Deleveraging
- Triggering event (financial shock, external stress, political crisis)
- Asset prices collapse across the board
- Debt restructuring cascade
- The question: deflation or inflation path?
  - Deflation path: default, austerity, depression (1930s US analog)
  - Inflation path: monetization, currency debasement (1970s US, or Weimar 1923)
  - "Beautiful deleveraging" (Dalio's preferred path): balance of all four tools

#### 2E — Restructuring / New Balance
- Debt wiped enough to restore solvency
- New growth cycle begins from clean balance sheet
- Often catalyzed by geopolitical reset (war/new order)

### Crisis Triggers (Long Cycle)
- Loss of confidence in the currency (foreign holders reduce exposure)
- Interest rates rising when debt too high to absorb (fiscal dominance)
- Political failure to address debt (gridlock, populism)
- Competing reserve currency emerges (reduces dollar demand)
- Geopolitical shock requiring massive military spending

### Indicators Per Stage

| Stage | Debt/GDP | Real Rates | Central Bank Tools | Asset Valuations | Inequality |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Early accumulation | 30-60% | Positive | Full toolkit | Moderate | Low |
| Mid expansion | 60-100% | Low/zero | Partial | Elevated | Rising |
| Late peak | 100-150%+ | Negative | QE only | Very high | Near highs |
| Crisis | Peaked then collapsing | Volatile | Exhausted | Collapsing | Highest then reset |
| Restructuring | Falling | Rising (real) | Rebuilding | Cheap | Compressed |

### Historical Examples
- **US 1920-1933**: Long cycle peak → Great Depression → deflation path (Roosevelt restructuring 1933-1940)
- **US 1945-present**: New cycle from Bretton Woods low debt base; now approaching peak (Dalio's thesis)
- **Japan 1990-present**: Long cycle peak in 1989; 30-year deflation path; still incomplete deleveraging
- **Weimar Germany 1919-1923**: Inflation/hyperinflation path — debt wiped via currency destruction

### Current Position (2024-2026 estimate)
Dalio framework: US likely in Stage 2C (late peak). Federal debt ~120% GDP; total economy debt ~350-360% GDP; Fed balance sheet >$7T; rates approaching zero lower bound again in next recession. The deleveraging question — deflation or inflation — is the central macro uncertainty of the next decade. *Framework application, not prediction.*

## Cycle 3 — Currency / Reserve Currency Cycle (~100-200 Years)

### Mechanism
Reserve currencies are established through military dominance, trade network centrality, and financial sophistication. They confer the "exorbitant privilege" — the ability to borrow cheaply in your own currency, run persistent deficits without immediate punishment, and seigniorage gains from global demand for your currency.

Reserve currencies fall when:
1. The issuing nation's fundamentals deteriorate (debt, military, economic)
2. A viable alternative emerges
3. Trust in the currency's store-of-value function collapses

This cycle is the slowest and most structural. Once a reserve currency begins declining, it typically takes 30-50 years to complete the transition.

### Stages

#### 3A — Establishment
- Rising power wins a decisive military/economic contest
- New monetary order created (gold standard, Bretton Woods, etc.)
- Dominant currency adopted for trade invoicing and reserve holding
- Financial center established in dominant nation
- **Indicators**: Currency share of reserves rising; commodity pricing switches to new currency

#### 3B — Prime (Peak Reserve Status)
- Currency universally accepted; near-zero risk premium
- Nation runs trade deficits without punishment (exports its currency, not goods)
- Financial innovation thrives (world's deepest capital markets in dominant currency)
- "Exorbitant privilege" fully operational
- **Indicators**: Currency 60-70%+ of global reserves; commodity pricing in dominant currency; most trade invoiced in dominant currency

#### 3C — Decline / Debasement
- Currency share of reserves begins declining
- Trust erodes due to debt monetization or political instability
- Rivals accumulate gold, alternative currencies, real assets
- Trade invoicing diversifies
- **Indicators**: Reserve share falling; gold price rising (in dominant currency); commodity pricing diversifying; SWIFT alternatives emerging

#### 3D — Crisis / Transition
- Sharp loss of confidence event (debt crisis, war, financial shock)
- Currency devaluation (sharp, not gradual)
- Former reserve currency becomes just another currency
- New reserve currency established or multi-polar system emerges

#### 3E — New Order
- New dominant currency established
- Former power maintains currency but loses privilege
- Structural adjustment (living standard decline, loss of borrowing advantage)

### Crisis Triggers (Currency Cycle)
- Geopolitical defeat or exhaustion (war overstretch)
- Debt burden so high that monetization becomes inevitable (fiscal dominance)
- Creditor nations losing confidence and diversifying reserves
- New power achieving critical mass in trade network, military, and financial systems

### Indicators Per Stage
| Stage | Reserve Share | Gold Price Trend | Trade Invoice % | Currency vs Gold |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Establishment | Rising to 40%+ | Falling (in new currency) | Switching to new | Strengthening |
| Prime | 60-70%+ | Stable/falling | Dominant | Stable/strong |
| Decline | 50-65%, falling | Rising | Diversifying | Weakening trend |
| Crisis | <50% sharply | Surging | Diversified | Sharp devaluation |
| New order | <30% | Stable in new currency | New dominant | Stabilized (weak) |

### Historical Examples
- **Dutch Guilder** (~1600-1720): Established via VOC trade dominance; declined as British power rose
- **British Pound** (~1815-1944): Established via industrial revolution + Pax Britannica; Bretton Woods ended dominance
  - Sterling share of reserves fell from ~64% (1900) to ~8% (1950) over 50 years
- **US Dollar** (~1945-present): Bretton Woods establishment; Nixon shock 1971 was stress point; still dominant but declining
  - USD share of reserves: 71% (2000) → 58% (2024)

**Dalio's estimated position**: USD is in Stage 3C (decline phase). Not imminent collapse, but secular trend downward. Could take 20-40 more years. *Framework interpretation of public Dalio statements.*

## Interaction Between the Three Cycles

The three cycles reinforce each other at critical junctures:

```
Short cycle peak (1C/1D) 
  → If long cycle also at peak (2C/2D): 
      → Central bank runs out of tools → monetization
      → If currency cycle also in decline (3C/3D):
          → Hard assets surge; fiat trust collapse
```

**Current convergence estimate (Dalio framework)**:
- Short cycle: Late 1C / early 1D (2024-2025)
- Long cycle: 2C (late peak; next downturn may be deleveraging event, not normal recession)
- Currency cycle: 3C (secular decline in progress)

This three-cycle convergence is why Dalio has been publicly warning about systemic risk since 2018, and why hard assets (gold, commodities) and non-dollar stores of value (Bitcoin in his framework) become strategically important in this window. *This is Dalio's published framework applied to available data — not a prediction.*
